Our understanding of the universe was fundamentally transformed by Albert Einstein's introduction of the theory of special relativity. This groundbreaking concept challenged and redefined our intuitive notions of space, time, and motion, marking the beginning of a new era in physics. Imagine a world where time and distance are not absolute, and where nothing can travel faster than the speed of light. This chapter will provide a foundational understanding of this mind-bending theory, which revolutionized our comprehension of the universe.

## 2.1 Introduction

Special relativity governs phenomena occurring at speeds approaching the speed of light, $ c = 3 \times 10^8 \ m/s$. It's important to recognize that nearly all speeds we encounter in everyday life are much slower than the speed of light. Even space rockets, which are among  the fastest human-made objects, travel at speeds no greater than  $25, 000 mi/h \approx 1.1 \times 10^4 \ m/s$. Consequently, our intuition about how objects behave at such extreme velocities is limited.

In the 1860s, the Scottish mathematical physicist James Clerk Maxwell (1831–1879) demonstrated that the speed of light in a vacuum is given by:


\begin{equation}
	c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} \nonumber
	\label{eg:0201}
\end{equation}

where $$\mu_0$$ is permeability of free space and $$\epsilon_0$$ is permittivity of free space.

At the time, physicists believed light, like other waves, required a medium for propagation, even in a vacuum. This hypothetical medium was called the ether. Maxwell proposed that the speed of light was constant relative to this ether. Scientists envisioned the ether as a delicate substance that allowed celestial bodies to pass through unimpeded. Therefore, it was expected that the measured speed of light would vary ($$ c \pm  v$$ ) depending on the direction of light travel relative to the ether.

To detect this variation, a rapidly moving frame of reference was needed. Earth's orbital velocity around the Sun ($$ 30 km/s \approx 3 \times  10^4 \ m/s$$) was considered sufficient. Assuming the Sun was at rest in the ether, this velocity could be used to measure changes in the speed of light.
**Transition to Modern Physics**
The transition from classical mechanics to modern physics marked a profound shift in our understanding of the universe. Pioneered by groundbreaking theories such as special relativity and quantum mechanics, modern physics revolutionized our comprehension of space, time, and matter. Albert Einstein's theories challenged classical notions, revealing the interplay between energy, mass, and the curvature of spacetime. Quantum mechanics, developed by luminaries like Planck and Schrödinger, unveiled the intricate behaviors of particles at the quantum level, introducing concepts like wave-particle duality and uncertainty.

Albert A. Michelson (1852-1931) was a distinguished American physicist of the 19th century. He is best known for his groundbreaking work in optics and the development of the interferometer, a highly precise instrument for measuring the phase difference between light waves. In recognition of his exceptional contributions, Michelson received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1907, becoming the first American to receive this prestigious honor in Physics.

In the late 19th century, scientists believed that light, like other waves, required a medium to propagate. This hypothetical medium was known as the ether, thought to permeate all of space. The Michelson-Morley experiment, conducted in 1887 by Michelson and American chemist Edward W. Morley (1838-1923), aimed to detect the Earth's motion through this ether by measuring variations in the speed of light. To their astonishment, the experiment yielded a null result, indicating no discernible difference in the speed of light regardless of the Earth's motion. This groundbreaking discovery challenged the prevailing ether theory and ultimately contributed to the development of Albert Einstein's groundbreaking theory of special relativity.

 In the following section, we will explore the experimental setup of the Michelson-Morley experiment in detail.

## 2.2 The Michelson-Morley Experiment
The Michelson-Morley experiment, conducted in 1887 by Albert A. Michelson and Edward W. Morley, stands as a pivotal experiment in the history of physics. It aimed to detect the existence of the "luminiferous ether," a hypothetical medium believed to permeate all of space and serve as the carrier for light waves.

As illustrated in figure 2, the Michelson interferometer consists of a light source, a beam splitter ($$M_0$$), two mirrors ($$M_1$$ and $$M_2$$), and a detector. Light from the source is split into two perpendicular paths by the beam splitter. Light from the source is divided by the beam splitter into two perpendicular paths. One beam travels to mirror $$M_1$$ and is reflected back to the beam splitter, while the other beam travels to mirror $$M_2$$ and is also reflected back. hese two beams are then recombined at the detector, where they produce an interference pattern (Fig-3). To minimize the impact of external vibrations, the entire apparatus was mounted on a stone slab and floated in a pool of mercury.

A key feature of the Michelson-Morley experiment was its exceptional sensitivity to changes in the optical path length of the light beams. This sensitivity was critical for detecting even subtle variations in the speed of light, which were expected if the ether existed.

To illustrate the interferometer's sensitivity, consider what happens when the distance between M<sub>0</sub>  and M<sub>1</sub> is halved relative to the length of the other arm (M<sub>0</sub> to M<sub>2 </sub>). In this case, a complete shift in the interference pattern occurs: bright fringes become dark, and dark fringes become bright. This dramatic change demonstrates the interferometer's remarkable ability to detect minuscule differences in the optical path length
